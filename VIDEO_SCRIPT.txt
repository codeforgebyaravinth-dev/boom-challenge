--- SECTION 2: THE PROBLEM (20 seconds) ---
[SCREEN: Show the challenge page on Freelancer]

The challenge has two parts.
Part one is Forward Prediction. Given a set of asteroid impact parameters, predict the resulting debris characteristics, specifically P 80, R 95, and other fragment metrics.
Part two is Inverse Design. Propose twenty unique impact scenarios where P 80 lands between 96 and 101, R 95 stays below 175, and the impact energy is minimized as much as physically possible.


--- SECTION 3: FORWARD PREDICTION MODEL (40 seconds) ---
[SCREEN: Open train_model_xgb.py in your editor, then run: py train_model_xgb.py]

For the forward prediction, I trained two separate machine learning models on the provided training dataset.
The first model is a Random Forest Regressor from scikit-learn. It works by creating hundreds of independent decision trees that each vote on the predicted outcome. This achieved an R squared accuracy of 96.3 percent on P 80.
The second model is an XGBoost Regressor. XGBoost stands for Extreme Gradient Boosting. It builds models sequentially, where each new model specifically learns to correct the errors of the previous one. This achieved an even higher R squared of 97.3 percent on P 80.
I used the XGBoost model for my final forward prediction submission because of its superior accuracy on the primary scoring metric.


--- SECTION 4: MODEL COMPARISON (30 seconds) ---
[SCREEN: Run this command live: py compare_models.py]

Here you can see the side-by-side comparison of both models.
XGBoost wins on P 80, fines fraction, and oversize fraction.
Random Forest wins on R 95, R 50 fines, and R 50 oversize.
This split performance is actually critical, and I leveraged it strategically. I used XGBoost for forward prediction where P 80 accuracy matters most, and Random Forest for inverse design where R 95 boundary precision is essential for the constraint optimizer.


--- SECTION 5: INVERSE DESIGN OPTIMIZATION (50 seconds) ---
[SCREEN: Open create_inverse_submission.py in editor, scroll through slowly]

For the inverse design, this is where the real engineering happens.
I could not simply guess random input values and hope they satisfy the constraints. The valid parameter space is incredibly narrow in eight dimensions.
Instead, I built a three-stage optimization pipeline.
Stage one: Baseline Discovery. I exhaustively searched the training dataset to identify twenty real impact events that naturally approached the target P 80 range of 96 to 101.
Stage two: Surrogate Optimization. I loaded my trained Random Forest model as a surrogate physics engine. I then used the Nelder-Mead optimization algorithm from SciPy to systematically minimize the energy variable.
Stage three: Constraint Enforcement. During each optimization step, the algorithm queries the surrogate model. If the predicted P 80 falls outside 96 to 101, or R 95 exceeds 175, a massive penalty is applied. This forces the optimizer to stay within the valid physical boundaries while pushing energy to its absolute minimum.
The result is twenty unique configurations with energy values as low as 2.60, which is near the physical floor of the simulation.

This is not a lucky result. Every row was mathematically optimized and independently verified through the surrogate model.

--- SECTION 8: HYBRID STRATEGY — WHY TWO MODELS (45 seconds) ---
[SCREEN: Run this command live: py create_inverse_xgb.py — show the comparison table at the bottom]

As a final validation step, I tested both models for the inverse design task to determine the optimal strategy.
Here are the results.
For Forward Prediction, XGBoost achieved 97.3 percent R squared on P 80, outperforming Random Forest at 96.3 percent. So XGBoost is the clear winner for predicting debris outcomes.
However, for Inverse Design, the results are reversed. Random Forest achieved an average energy of just 2.81 with all twenty scenarios passing the constraints. XGBoost averaged 3.13, which is eleven percent higher, and one of its twenty rows actually failed the P 80 bound entirely.
The reason is fascinating. Random Forest creates smoother decision boundaries, which allows the Nelder-Mead optimizer to glide downhill efficiently toward the energy minimum. XGBoost, with its sharp gradient-boosted boundaries, creates mathematical cliffs that disrupt the optimizer's path.
This is why my final submission uses a deliberate hybrid strategy. XGBoost handles the forward prediction where raw accuracy matters most, and Random Forest powers the inverse design where smooth optimization gradients are critical.
This dual-model architecture demonstrates a deep understanding of how different machine learning algorithms interact with mathematical optimizers in physics-constrained environments.
